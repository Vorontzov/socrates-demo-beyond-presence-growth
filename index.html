Opening Questions

â–¶ What was Eli Lilly's CSR dilemma in 2003?
Context

In 2003, Eli Lilly faced a critical strategic decision between two major CSR initiatives:

  • Deepen MDR-TB Commitment: Continue supporting WHO's Green Light Committee to combat multi-drug-resistant tuberculosis, which kills 8,000 people daily. Required building new manufacturing plants with unclear ROI.
  • Expand Global Diabetes Programs: Scale up programs like the Ghana pilot and "Life for a Child" initiative, leveraging Lilly's core competency in diabetes ($2.13B revenue, 86% US insulin market share).

The dilemma balanced humanitarian leadership vs. core business alignment amid industry-wide criticism of pharmaceutical pricing.

â–¶ Who is Sidney Taurel and what challenges did he face?
Leadership

Sidney Taurel was CEO and Chairman of Eli Lilly in 2003, leading the company during turbulent times:

  • Financial pressure: Prozac patent expiration caused sales to drop, requiring new growth strategies
  • Industry criticism: Pharmaceutical companies faced lawsuits and public anger over drug pricing (e.g., 39-company South Africa lawsuit)
  • Innovation imperative: Only 153 of 1,035 new drugs (1989-2000) were truly innovative; industry R&D questioned
  • Moral conviction: Taurel believed "we do have a moral responsibility" to save lives, creating tension between ethics and shareholder returns
â–¶ Why was the pharmaceutical industry under fire?
Industry

The pharmaceutical industry faced mounting criticism on multiple fronts:

  • Drug pricing: HIV/AIDS drugs priced at $10,000+ per year were unaffordable in developing countries; 39 companies sued South Africa over generic production
  • Patent enforcement: WTO TRIPS agreement (1995) required developing countries to enforce drug patents, blocking access to cheap generics
  • R&D priorities: Critics argued pharma focused on "me-too" drugs and lifestyle medications instead of life-threatening diseases
  • Medicare debates: US seniors faced high drug costs; political pressure for price controls increased
  • Public perception: Seen as prioritizing profits over lives, especially in global health crises

Strategic Analysis

â–¶ What are the pros and cons of the MDR-TB initiative?
Strategy

PROS:

  • Humanitarian impact: Addresses 8,000 deaths/day; 400,000 MDR-TB cases annually
  • Scientific differentiation: Capreomycin and cycloserine are complex fermentation products with no viable generic competitors
  • Partnership credibility: Builds on proven WHO GLC success in Chile, Peru, Philippines
  • Regulatory goodwill: "CSR helps us dramatically with our regulators" (Pfizer Chairman)
  • Industry competitiveness: Matches Novartis Singapore center, GSK $200M Spain facility

CONS:

  • Major CapEx: Requires tens of millions for new plant construction
  • Declining sales: Anti-infectives sales dropped 23% to $577M (2002)
  • Uncertain ROI: Drugs supplied at ~1% market price to WHO; minimal revenue
  • Manufacturing complexity: 2001 FDA shutdowns exposed fermentation risks
â–¶ What are the pros and cons of diabetes programs?
Strategy

PROS:

  • Core competency: $2.13B diabetes revenue; 86% US insulin market share
  • Growth trajectory: Diabetes population projected to double (150M â†’ 300M by 2025)
  • Strong margins: EBITDA 36.3%, ROE 42.3%
  • CEO alignment: Taurel's focus on chronic conditions
  • Proven model: $1M Ghana pilot; IDF partnerships; "Life for a Child" program ($365/child/year)
  • Low incremental cost: Leverages existing products and distribution

CONS:

  • Less urgent crisis: Not an 8,000 deaths/day emergency like MDR-TB
  • Infrastructure challenges: Requires refrigeration, patient compliance
  • Competitive: Multiple pharma companies in diabetes space
  • Moderate PR impact: Less dramatic than infectious disease leadership
â–¶ Which option better aligns with Lilly's core strategy?
Strategic Fit

Diabetes programs show stronger strategic alignment:

  • Revenue focus: 2 of 6 growth products are diabetes drugs; represents 18% of total sales
  • CEO priority: Taurel explicitly focuses on chronic conditions, not infectious disease
  • Market growth: Expanding diabetes population creates long-term business opportunity
  • Low risk: No plant construction; leverages existing manufacturing and supply chains
  • Profitability: High margins support both business returns and CSR investment

However, MDR-TB offers differentiation:

  • Heritage: Lilly's historical strength in anti-infectives (penicillin, erythromycin)
  • Scientific moat: Complex fermentation expertise few competitors possess
  • Reputational leadership: More dramatic humanitarian positioning vs. competitors

Financial Impact

â–¶ What are the financial implications of MDR-TB?
Finance

High upfront costs, minimal revenue:

  • CapEx burden: Tens of millions required for new manufacturing facilities
  • Pricing reality: Drugs supplied to WHO at ~1% of market price; negligible revenue contribution
  • Declining segment: Anti-infectives sales fell 23% (2001-2002), down to $577M from peak
  • Idle capacity utilization: Could repurpose existing fermentation plants, reducing incremental cost
  • Intangible benefits: Regulatory goodwill, brand equity, employee morale (hard to quantify)
  • Risk exposure: Manufacturing complexity (2001 FDA shutdowns) creates compliance liability

Net impact: Significant CSR investment (~$251M annual budget could absorb some cost) with strategic, not financial, returns.

â–¶ What is the business case for diabetes programs?
Business Case

Strong financial and strategic alignment:

  • Revenue opportunity: $2.13B current diabetes sales (18% of total); projected market doubling by 2025 (150M â†’ 300M patients)
  • High profitability: EBITDA margin 36.3%, ROE 42.3% support both growth and CSR funding
  • Low incremental cost: Ghana pilot ran $1M over 4 years; "Life for a Child" costs $365/child/year
  • Market expansion: Building awareness in developing markets creates future customer base as economies grow
  • Brand halo effect: Strengthens Lilly's diabetes leadership positioning globally
  • Risk mitigation: No new manufacturing; leverages existing supply chains and partner networks (140 alliances)

ROI potential: CSR investment doubles as market development, with measurable patient outcomes justifying continued funding.

Stakeholder Perspectives

â–¶ How would shareholders view each option?
Stakeholders

Shareholder priorities (ROE 42.3%, seeking growth after Prozac patent loss):

  • Favor diabetes: Aligns with growth strategy; leverages core competency; expanding market; visible ROI path
  • Skeptical of MDR-TB: Major CapEx in declining segment; minimal revenue; uncertain strategic payoff; manufacturing risk
  • CSR tolerance: $251M annual philanthropy (7.22% of pre-tax profits) sets precedent; shareholders accept reasonable CSR spending
  • Reputation hedge: May support MDR-TB if positioned as protecting regulatory relationships and brand value vs. industry criticism

Likely verdict: Diabetes seen as "smart CSR" (business + ethics); MDR-TB requires compelling non-financial case.

â–¶ What would patients in developing countries want?
Stakeholders

Depends on disease burden and urgency:

  • MDR-TB patients: Facing imminent death (8,000 daily); desperate for treatment access; capreomycin/cycloserine are life-saving; demand immediate action
  • Diabetes patients: Chronic condition requiring long-term management; equally desperate but different urgency; need sustained insulin supply, refrigeration, education
  • Infrastructure reality: Both face barriersâ€”TB needs DOTS compliance; diabetes needs cold chain, monitoring, lifestyle change
  • Economic access: Both groups lack ability to pay Western prices; rely on public health systems, NGOs, donation programs

Patient voice: "Any help is better than none"â€”but TB patients face existential crisis now; diabetes patients seek long-term partnership.

Partnerships

Critical partners with strong preferences:

  • WHO Green Light Committee: Already partnered with Lilly on MDR-TB; proven model in Chile, Peru, Philippines; urgently needs scaled production; would champion deeper commitment
  • International Diabetes Federation (IDF): Partnered on Ghana pilot; advocates for global diabetes access; supports expansion but less urgent crisis framing
  • MÃ©decins Sans FrontiÃ¨res (MSF): Vocal on drug access; would applaud either but favor MDR-TB given acute mortality and Lilly's unique production capability

Ethical Considerations

â–¶ What are the ethical dimensions of this case?
Ethics

Fundamental tensions between ethical frameworks:

  • Utilitarian view: Maximize lives savedâ€”MDR-TB addresses 8,000 deaths/day (immediate impact); diabetes prevents long-term suffering (broader reach)
  • Duty-based ethics: Lilly has unique capability to produce complex TB drugs (moral obligation); also has expertise in diabetes (competing duty)
  • Justice/equity: Poorest patients need both drugs; pricing locks them out; CSR partially corrects market failure but can't solve systemic access crisis
  • Stakeholder theory: Balance shareholder returns, patient needs, employee values, regulatory relationshipsâ€”no option satisfies all
  • Virtue ethics: What would a "good" pharmaceutical company do? Heritage of discovery (Colonel Lilly's mass-production of insulin) vs. modern profit pressures

Core question: Is pharma's primary obligation to shareholders or society? Taurel's "moral responsibility" challenges shareholder primacy doctrine.

â–¶ Should pharma prioritize profit or access?
Ethics

The central ethical debate of pharmaceutical business:

PROFIT PRIORITY (Shareholder primacy view):

  • High R&D costs (~$800M per new drug) require revenue to sustain innovation
  • Profits fund future drug discovery; "no margin, no mission"
  • Patent protection incentivizes risk-taking; without IP, innovation stalls
  • Market mechanisms drive efficiency; governments/NGOs should fund access

ACCESS PRIORITY (Social obligation view):

  • Health is a human right; life-saving drugs shouldn't be priced out of reach
  • Pharma benefits from public R&D (NIH funding); owes society return
  • Tiered pricing/licensing allows profit in rich markets + access in poor markets
  • Industry reputation collapse threatens long-term sustainability (see HIV/AIDS lawsuit backlash)

Middle ground: "Shared value" modelâ€”CSR programs that build markets, reputation, and relationships while serving unmet medical needs. Both diabetes and MDR-TB fit this, but with different risk/reward profiles.

  • Example split: 60% diabetes ($150M) + 40% MDR-TB ($100M)

  • 140 existing alliance partnerships provide distribution infrastructure

ADVANTAGES:

  • Hedges strategic betâ€”gains benefits of both approaches
  • Satisfies diverse stakeholdersâ€”WHO/NGOs (TB), shareholders (diabetes)
  • Builds comprehensive "global health leadership" brand
  • Allows learning and scaling winners over time

RISKS:

  • Diluted focusâ€”neither program reaches transformative scale
  • MDR-TB still requires major CapEx; can't be done "at half scale"
  • Message confusionâ€”lacks narrative clarity vs. single bold bet

Debate this: Is "both" strategic wisdom or a failure to choose? Does it maximize impact or minimize it?

Key Figures & Data

  • MDR-TB cases: 400,000 of 8 million TB cases annually
  • Diabetes patients: 150M globally (2003), predicted 300M by 2025

Treatment Costs:

  • MDR-TB treatment (US): $250,000 per patient
  • Standard TB treatment: $2,000 per patient
  • Diabetes care (Ghana pilot): $365 per child/year
  • Insulin (US retail): $49.84 per 30-day supply (average)
  • Insulin (Lilly Answers program): $12 per 30-day supply
  • AIDS treatment (US patented): $10,000 annually
  • After Accelerating Access: ~$1,000
  • Generic alternative (Cipla): $600

MDR-TB Initiative Details

  • Target disease: Multi-drug resistant tuberculosis (8,000 deaths/day)
  • Lilly drugs: Capreomycin and cycloserine (complex fermentation products)
  • Partnership model: WHO Green Light Committee (GLC) with NGOs
  • Proven locations: Chile, Peru, Philippines, Estonia
  • Pricing: Supplied to WHO at ~1% of market price
  • Challenge: Requires tens of millions in new plant construction
  • Market context: Anti-infectives sales declined 23% (2001-2002)

Diabetes Initiative Details

  • Target disease: Diabetes mellitus (150M patients globally, doubling by 2025)
  • Lilly position: 86% US insulin market share; $2.13B revenue (18% of total sales)
  • Partnership model: International Diabetes Federation (IDF); local health ministries
  • Proven location: Ghana pilot program ($1M over 4 years)
  • Key program: "Life for a Child" initiative ($365/child/year)
  • Advantage: Leverages existing products, no new manufacturing required
  • Market context: 2 of Lilly's 6 growth products are diabetes drugs
  • Margins: EBITDA 36.3%, ROE 42.3% support CSR investment

Key Concepts & Terms

  • CSR: Corporate Social Responsibility
  • MDR-TB: Multi-drug resistant tuberculosis
  • TRIPS: Trade Related Aspects of Intellectual Property Rights (WTO, 1995)
  • DOTS-Plus: Directly Observed Treatment Short-course (TB treatment protocol)
  • Green Light Committee (GLC): WHO-led partnership platform (Lilly, NGOs, governments)
  • Compulsory Licensing: Government authorization to produce patented drug without permission
  • IDF: International Diabetes Federation (global advocacy organization)
  • Tiered Pricing: Different drug prices for different markets based on ability to pay
  • Access Programs: Patient assistance initiatives (e.g., Lilly Cares, Lilly Answers)
